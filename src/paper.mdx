## Abstract

We investigate whether structured mathematical knowledge helps LLMs reason and explore mathematics more effectively. We introduce **MathAgent**, a multi-agent system that augments LLM theorem provers with a semantically enriched knowledge graph (**MathKG**: 364 verified theorems, 9,434 typed edges across 7 domains) and Mathlib library retrieval. Evaluated across 1,520 benchmark problems from miniF2F, PutnamBench, and MathOlympiadBench with multiple models (Claude Sonnet 4.6, Goedel-Prover-V2-32B, Qwen3-8B), we find a striking **complementarity effect**: knowledge augmentation solves *different* problems than unaugmented proving, expanding the frontier of provable theorems by 18-53% through union of strategies. We further evaluate on 134 open OEIS conjectures and 9 Erdos problems solved by DeepMind's AlphaProof Nexus, identifying monolithic proof generation and computational decidability barriers as fundamental limitations of current single-shot approaches.

*This is an ongoing research project. Results and content are being actively updated.*

</HighlightedSection>

## Key Findings

- **Complementarity effect**: Different knowledge sources (KG, Mathlib retrieval) solve different problems. Combining all strategies yields 18-53% more solved problems than any single approach.

- **Knowledge substitution hypothesis**: External knowledge augmentation is most valuable for models near their capability frontier. Lean-specialized models (Goedel-V2-32B) gain less from augmentation but still benefit on specific hard problems.

- **Cross-model validation**: On MathOlympiadBench, Goedel-V2-32B solves 16/360 problems (4.4%) with prover-only, while KG uniquely solves 3 additional IMO problems (including Imo1988P3 and Imo2000P2) that prover-only cannot reach.

- **Open problem exploration**: On genuinely unsolved problems (OEIS conjectures, Erdos problems), we identify that the key bottleneck is monolithic proof generation— not knowledge availability — motivating iterative proof construction as future work.

## System Overview

  <span slot="caption">The MathAgent five-agent architecture. MathKG serves as persistent shared memory. An Orchestrator (not shown) coordinates the discovery loop: Explorer → Conjecturer → Prover → Integrator → Explorer..., with each verified theorem enriching the graph for subsequent iterations.</span>

MathAgent consists of five agents:

1. **Explorer** — Navigates the knowledge graph and retrieves relevant Mathlib declarations
2. **Conjecturer** — Generates formal conjectures grounded in KG structure
3. **Prover** — Generates Lean 4 tactic proofs with compiler feedback
4. **Integrator** — Validates and integrates proven theorems back into the KG
5. **Orchestrator** — Coordinates the pipeline across four evaluation modes

### Evaluation Modes

| Mode | Knowledge Source | Description |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| `prover_only` | None | Baseline: LLM proves from theorem statement alone |
| `with_kg` | MathKG | KG-guided: relevant theorems retrieved via semantic edges |
| `with_mathlib` | Mathlib | Retrieval-augmented: keyword search over 100K+ declarations |
| `full_system` | Both | Full pipeline: KG + Mathlib retrieval combined |

### Benchmarks

We evaluate on three established formal mathematics benchmarks:

- **miniF2F** (488 problems) — High-school and undergraduate competition mathematics formalized in Lean 4
- **PutnamBench** (672 problems) — Problems from the William Lowell Putnam Mathematical Competition
- **MathOlympiadBench** (360 problems) — International Mathematical Olympiad and regional competition problems

For open-ended exploration, we additionally evaluate on:

- **OEIS Conjectures** (134 problems) — Open conjectures about integer sequences from the Formal Conjectures repository
- **Erdos Problems** (335 problems) — Open number theory and combinatorics conjectures from the same repository

### Complementarity: Union of Strategies

<Figure>
  <Picture slot="figure" src="../assets/plot_venn_complementarity.pdf" alt="Venn diagram showing complementarity across augmentation modes" />
  <span slot="caption">Complementarity across augmentation modes (Sonnet 4.6, miniF2F). Different modes solve different problems — the union of all strategies significantly expands the frontier of provable theorems.</span>
</Figure>

The Venn diagram reveals our central finding: knowledge augmentation does not simply improve or hurt performance uniformly — it shifts *which* problems become solvable. This complementarity suggests that adaptive strategy selection (choosing augmentation based on problem characteristics) could outperform any fixed approach.

The key insight is not aggregate solve rates but the *union* of solved problems across modes:

- **Sonnet 4.6**: Union across all 4 modes solves 29-53% more problems than any single mode
- **Goedel-V2-32B**: Union of prover_only + with_kg solves 19 problems (18% more than best single mode of 16)
- **Qwen3-8B**: KG provides +9% relative improvement on miniF2F, consistent with knowledge substitution hypothesis

### Open-Ended Exploration: Pushing Beyond Benchmarks

We additionally evaluate MathAgent on genuinely unsolved problems from DeepMind's Formal Conjectures repository — the same dataset used by AlphaProof Nexus. Our system uses error-guided retry (pass@32 for Erdos, pass@64 for OEIS), where each failed attempt's compiler error is fed back to guide the next proof attempt.

**Key findings from 134 OEIS conjectures** (Goedel-V2-32B):
- 80%+ of attempts reach "unsolved goals" — meaning the proof *structure* (induction, case splits, intermediate lemmas) is correct, but the final mathematical step cannot be discharged
- The model generates 53–59 line proofs on average with proper `have...by` nesting, induction, and Mathlib API usage
- Knowledge augmentation drives diverse proof strategies (different modes attempt different mathematical approaches) but does not bridge the final gap

**What we learned — the capability frontier hypothesis:**
Our error-guided retry loop provides compiler feedback between attempts, but each attempt must produce a *complete* proof from scratch. The model cannot preserve partially-correct subproofs across retries. This contrasts with AlphaProof Nexus's iterative refinement, where proof sketches with `sorry` holes are verified for structural correctness, then individual subgoals are independently refined. We find that knowledge augmentation helps when problems are near the model's capability frontier (as on benchmarks), but cannot substitute for architectural advances in proof construction when problems exceed that frontier.
